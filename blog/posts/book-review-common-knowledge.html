The Power of Common Knowledge: When Everyone Knows That Everyone Knows

Why information isn't enough—and how shared awareness transforms individual knowledge into collective action.

LF

Lee Foropoulos

10 min read

There's a concept from game theory that changed how I think about organizational change, market dynamics, and even social movements: common knowledge. It's not just about what people know—it's about what people know that other people know, and what they know that others know that they know, recursively.

This might sound academic, but the practical implications are profound. Let me explain with an example.

The Emperor's New Clothes Problem

In the classic fairy tale, everyone in the crowd individually knows the emperor is naked. But nobody acts on this knowledge because they don't know that everyone else also knows. They assume they might be wrong, or alone in their perception.

Then a child shouts "He's naked!" and everything changes. Not because the child added new information—everyone already knew—but because the child's announcement made that knowledge common. Now everyone knows that everyone knows. And suddenly, coordinated action becomes possible.

"Common knowledge isn't about information. It's about the mutual awareness of information that enables coordination."

Why This Matters for Business

Market Movements and Bubbles

Asset bubbles often persist not because investors believe the prices are justified, but because each investor believes other investors believe it. The bubble pops when common knowledge shifts—when everyone suddenly knows that everyone knows the prices are unsustainable.

This is why public statements from credible figures can move markets dramatically. It's not the information itself (often already known); it's the creation of common knowledge.

Organizational Change

I've seen this pattern repeatedly in companies struggling with transformation. Everyone might privately know that a particular strategy isn't working, or that a leader is ineffective, or that the culture is toxic. But nothing changes because people don't know that others share their perception.

This is why town halls, all-hands meetings, and public acknowledgments from leadership matter so much during change initiatives. They create common knowledge.

Team Dynamics

Team dysfunction often stems from common knowledge failures. Someone isn't performing, and everyone knows it—but everyone assumes they're the only one who's noticed, or that leadership doesn't know. The result is paralysis.

Effective leaders make relevant knowledge common. They say out loud what people are thinking privately. This isn't about gossip or blame; it's about creating the shared awareness necessary for coordinated action.

The Coordination Problem

Common knowledge is essential for solving coordination problems—situations where the best outcome requires multiple people to act together, but no individual wants to act alone.

Think about:

  • Network effects: No one wants to join an empty social network, but everyone would join if everyone else joined.
  • Standing ovations: People wait to see if others stand before committing.
  • Workplace initiatives: New processes fail if individuals don't believe others will adopt them.
  • Market standards: VHS vs. Betamax wasn't about quality; it was about coordination.

In all these cases, individual knowledge isn't enough. What matters is shared certainty that others will act.

How to Create Common Knowledge

1. Public Announcements Over Private Conversations

If you need coordinated action, make the relevant information public. A team-wide email creates more common knowledge than individual conversations, even if the content is identical.

2. Rituals and Ceremonies

There's a reason cultures develop rituals. A graduation ceremony doesn't add information—the student already passed. But it creates common knowledge in the community that this person has achieved a milestone.

Company kickoffs, product launches, and celebration events serve similar functions.

3. Visible Metrics and Dashboards

When everyone can see the same numbers, you create common knowledge about performance. This is why transparent metrics drive accountability more effectively than private reports.

4. Credible Signals

Sometimes you need a trusted third party to establish common knowledge. This is why external audits, certifications, and press coverage matter—they create shared awareness that internal statements can't.

The Dark Side

Common knowledge can also be weaponized. Propaganda works by creating false common knowledge—making people believe that everyone believes something. Authoritarian regimes suppress free assembly and open communication precisely because these create common knowledge that could enable coordination against the regime.

In organizations, toxic leaders sometimes maintain power by preventing the formation of common knowledge. They isolate critics, discourage open discussion, and control information flows.

Practical Takeaways

  • For leaders: Your job isn't just to know things or to inform individuals—it's to create shared awareness that enables coordination.
  • For change agents: Private dissent doesn't create change. Public acknowledgment of problems does.
  • For strategists: When analyzing why coordination fails, ask not what people know, but what they know that others know.
  • For communicators: The venue and visibility of a message matters as much as its content.

Further Reading

The academic treatment of common knowledge comes from game theorist Robert Aumann. For more accessible explorations, I recommend:

  • Rational Ritual by Michael Suk-Young Chwe
  • Ben Hunt's writing at Epsilon Theory on narrative and common knowledge in markets
  • Scott Alexander's essays on coordination problems

Key Insight

Information alone doesn't drive collective action. Common knowledge—the shared awareness that everyone knows what everyone knows—is the catalyst for coordination.
